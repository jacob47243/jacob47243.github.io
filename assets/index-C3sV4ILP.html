Dynamic RRT Algorithms

A comparison of dynamic path-planning variants that replan in real time as new obstacles appear, including Dynamic RRT*, RRT*-FND, Dual-Tree RRT*, Density-Based Dual-Tree RRT*, and Bidirectional RRT*. We evaluate convergence time, path length, node count, and number of replans across multiple maps.

Dynamic RRT*

Retains useful portions of the tree and replans only from the last collision-free node when new obstacles are discovered—faster than rebuilding from scratch.

RRT*-FND (Fixed-Node Dynamic)

Trims only invalid edges, reconnects existing nodes, and maintains a fixed node budget by pruning least-useful leaves—reduces overhead and memory growth.

Dual-Tree RRT*

Grows two trees from start and goal; on collision, switches which side grows and aims the active tree toward the last safe node of the other.

Density-Based Dual-Tree RRT*

Selects the sparser of the two trees to grow after collisions to encourage uniform exploration and avoid wasted work in already dense regions.

Bidirectional RRT*

Expands both trees every iteration and favors connections between them—often the fastest to converge in cluttered spaces.

Results (high level)

  • Bidirectional RRT* typically showed the lowest convergence time by growing two trees concurrently.
  • Dual-Tree & Density-Based Dual-Tree often produced shorter paths than the baseline naive approach, thanks to richer coverage near obstacles.
  • Naive RRT had the longest paths on average, since it replans from scratch and tends to avoid tight corridors around new obstacles.
